# MindCraft — Viability Research Strategy
### How to Talk to Students, Parents & Tutors Before Building More

---

## Why This Matters Now

You have a working product. That's rare. But the risk at this stage isn't technical — it's building the wrong thing confidently. Before you invest more engineering time, you need to answer three questions that no dashboard can answer:

1. **Who actually has the problem badly enough to pay or evangelize?**
2. **What does "a good tutoring experience" mean emotionally, not just functionally?**
3. **What would make a parent trust and keep paying for this platform vs. the alternatives?**

The form you built is a good start for desirability signals. This doc turns it into a real research plan.

---

## The Three Stakeholders & What You Need From Each

### 1. Students (Ages 14–22)
**What you need to understand:**
- When do they feel most stuck — and what do they do in that moment?
- What makes them actually show up prepared vs. ghosting?
- What would make them tell a friend about MindCraft?

**Emotional territory to probe:**
- Academic shame (feeling dumb, not wanting to ask "obvious" questions)
- Performance anxiety vs. genuine curiosity
- Social proof — do their peers use tutoring or is it stigmatized?

**Where to find them:**
- Macalester campus green, library, dorm common areas
- Coffee shops near campus (Black Coffee & Waffle Bar, Dunn Bros on Grand)
- High school near you — ask a teacher to introduce you to a small group after class
- Discord study servers (r/learnmath, subject-specific)

### 2. Parents (of students ages 12–18)
**What you need to understand:**
- What triggers the decision to hire a tutor? (Crisis moment? Proactive investment?)
- How do they evaluate whether tutoring is "working"?
- What would make them trust a platform they haven't heard of?
- Who else competes for this budget? (Kumon, Wyzant, Khan Academy, private tutors)

**Emotional territory to probe:**
- Anxiety about their child falling behind
- Skepticism about "another app"
- The parent-child communication gap around academics
- ROI thinking — they want to see improvement, not just sessions

**Where to find them:**
- Door-to-door in residential neighborhoods near Macalester or St. Paul high schools
  (Summit Ave, Crocus Hill, Mac-Groveland are high-density families)
- Parent pickup zones at high schools (3–4pm)
- NextDoor app — post a request for 10-min conversations
- Local Facebook parent groups for St. Paul / Minneapolis schools
- After-school program waiting areas

### 3. Tutors (College students, recent grads, professionals)
**What you need to understand:**
- What makes them take or reject a student?
- What admin work do they hate most?
- What would make them feel proud of a session?
- Would they pay for a platform? Refer others to it?

**Emotional territory to probe:**
- Desire for impact vs. just earning money
- Frustration with scheduling, no-shows, unclear expectations
- Identity — do they see tutoring as a career or a side gig?

**Where to find them:**
- Macalester tutoring center, TA offices
- Flyer on department bulletin boards: "10-min conversation, $5 coffee gift card"
- LinkedIn search: "tutor" + your city
- Facebook groups for college students looking for side income

## Critique of Your Current Form

### What's Working
- The format is low-friction
- Open-ended closing questions ("what would make you proud") will yield gold
- Covering multiple user types in one form is clever for early signal gathering

### What's Missing / Needs Rethinking

#### Problem 1 — Too Much Selection, Not Enough Story
Multiple-choice answers tell you *what* people picked, not *why*. The richest insight comes from "tell me about a time when..." questions.

**Replace or supplement:**
> "When it gets serious, I [Google, review notes, use AI...]"

**With:**
> "Tell me about the last time you really struggled with something in school. Walk me through exactly what you did."

This surfaces the actual behavior, not the aspirational one.

---

#### Problem 2 — The JARVIS/Sherlock/Polymath question is product-led, not problem-led
You're asking people to evaluate your solution before confirming they have the problem. This creates false positives — people say "JARVIS sounds cool" but would never actually use it.

**Replace:**
> "J.A.R.V.I.S. an integrated system with all your notes, flashcards, practice questions..."

**With:**
> "What's the biggest gap between how you study now and how you wish you could study?" (open text)

Then use what they say to validate whether JARVIS actually solves it.

---

#### Problem 3 — "I like helping friends with studying because..." is asking for rationalization
People will pick the most flattering answer. This tells you nothing about actual tutor motivation.

**Replace with:**
> "Have you ever tutored or helped someone with schoolwork? What made you keep doing it — or stop?"

---

#### Problem 4 — Parent section is too abstract
"What would make you trust a tutoring platform" is too big a question cold. Parents need context to answer meaningfully.

**Better sequence:**
1. "How did you find your child's last tutor?"
2. "How did you know whether it was working?"
3. "What made you stop using them — or what would make you stop?"
4. "What would a tutor need to show you in the first 30 days to earn your continued trust?"

---

#### Problem 5 — No questions about switching cost or alternatives
You need to understand what people are already doing and why they'd leave it.

**Add:**
- "What do you use right now for academic support?" (open text)
- "What would have to be true for you to switch to something new?"
- "How much do you currently spend on tutoring per month?" (range options)

## Better Questions to Add

### For Students
1. Walk me through the last time you felt really lost in a class. What did you actually do?
2. If a friend recommended a tutor, what's the first thing you'd check before saying yes?
3. What's the thing about studying you wish you could just make disappear?
4. Have you ever had a tutoring session that felt like a waste of time? What made it that way?
5. If you had an extra free hour today, would you use it to study? Why or why not?

### For Parents
1. How did your child's last academic struggle start — and how did you find out about it?
2. Walk me through how you found the tutor you're using now (or last used).
3. What does "my child is making progress" actually look like to you?
4. If a tutor sent you a summary after every session, what would need to be in it?
5. What would make you recommend a tutoring platform to another parent without being asked?

### For Tutors
1. What's the most annoying part of setting up a new student relationship?
2. Tell me about a session that went really well. What made it that way?
3. If a platform handled all your scheduling and payment, what else would you still stress about?
4. How do you currently track your students' progress? What's missing from that process?
5. What would make you feel like tutoring was worth more than just money?

## Research Protocol — How to Run the Conversations

### Format
- **Target:** 5 conversations per stakeholder group to start (15 total)
- **Length:** 20–30 minutes max. Don't go over.
- **Incentive:** $5–10 coffee gift card or just buy them a coffee
- **Record:** Ask permission, use Voice Memos on your phone
- **Note:** Don't take notes during — it breaks eye contact. Transcribe after.

### The Opening Script (use this verbatim)
> "I'm building a tutoring platform and I'm trying to understand people's real experiences before I build anything else. I'm not here to sell you anything. I just want to learn from you for 20 minutes. There are no right or wrong answers — the more honest the better. Is that okay?"

### The Rules
1. **Never ask leading questions.** "Do you find it hard to stay consistent?" → bad. "Tell me about your study habits" → good.
2. **Follow the silence.** When someone stops talking, wait 3 seconds before speaking. Gold often comes after the pause.
3. **Ask "why" three times.** If someone says "I just want better grades" — ask why that matters, then why again. You'll hit the real emotional driver.
4. **Don't defend your product.** If someone says tutoring apps are useless, say "tell me more about that" not "but ours is different."

## What to Do With What You Learn

After 15 conversations, do this:

### Step 1 — Affinity Mapping (1 hour)
Write every notable quote on a sticky note (physical or Miro). Group them by theme without labeling the themes first. Labels emerge from the clusters.

### Step 2 — Find the Jobs to Be Done
For each stakeholder, fill in:
> "When _______, I want to _______, so I can _______."

Example: "When my child fails a test, I want to understand what went wrong immediately, so I can feel like I'm doing something about it."

This drives product decisions better than any feature list.

### Step 3 — Score Your Current Features
For each feature you've built (dashboard, transcript review, session summary, chat), ask:
- Which job does this serve?
- Did anyone mention wanting this unprompted?
- Would someone pay for this specifically?

### Step 4 — Kill / Keep / Build
- **Kill** anything nobody mentioned needing
- **Keep** anything that maps to a real emotional job
- **Build** only what came up across 3+ conversations unprompted

## The One Thing to Remember

The goal of this research is not to validate that people like your idea. It's to find the **one or two moments of genuine pain** that are sharp enough that someone would change their behavior to fix them. That's your wedge. Everything else is polish.

Go find the person who is most frustrated. Build for them first.
